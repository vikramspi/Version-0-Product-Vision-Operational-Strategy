Version 0 — Product Vision & Operational Strategy

Five purchases.
Five websites. One unified order.

Furnishing a home today means juggling five separate purchases across five separate websites, five separate deliveries, and five separate installation calls. This platform collapses that into one: a customer describes their space, receives AI-curated recommendations mapped to real, purchasable products, and experiences delivery, installation, and support as a single coordinated journey — regardless of how many brands actually fulfill the order behind the scenes.

01 Executive Summary

The value proposition isn't "AI shopping."
It's unification.

The AI is the discovery mechanism; the unified cart-to-installation experience is what customers retain and pay for, because it's harder to copy than a recommendation engine. Anyone can suggest a sofa. Installing that sofa alongside a TV and a wardrobe from three brands, in one visit, with one point of accountability, is an operational moat.

🛋️
The Fragmentation Problem
Furnishing a home today means juggling five separate purchases across five separate websites, five separate deliveries, and five separate installation calls — with no single party accountable for the outcome.
🧭
The Platform's Job
A customer describes their space, receives AI-curated recommendations mapped to real, purchasable products, and experiences delivery, installation, and support as one coordinated journey — regardless of how many brands fulfill the order behind the scenes.
🏰
The Operational Moat
Anyone can suggest a sofa. Installing that sofa alongside a TV and a wardrobe from three brands, in one visit, with one point of accountability, is what's actually hard to copy.
02 Product Philosophy

Four principles the entire product is built on.

Every later decision in this document — what's in V0, which delivery model wins, how exceptions are handled — traces back to one of these four.

🧠
AI reduces decisions, not increases them
One strong recommendation with 3–4 real alternatives — never an open-ended catalog with an AI veneer.
🧾
One customer, one order, one experience
One cart, one checkout, one tracking view, one support thread — no matter how many brands or couriers or installers are involved.
🛒
Marketplace with managed experience
The platform owns the experience layer, not inventory — discovery and fulfillment coordination, where existing marketplaces leave things fragmented.
📐
Build the smallest version that proves the promise
V0 isn't the smartest version of this product — it's the smallest one that makes "one unified journey" undeniably real.
03 Product Overview

09 Delivery Strategy Comparison

Three delivery models. One delivers the unified promise at V0 cost.

Recommended: Model C, Hybrid Scheduling. It's the only model that delivers the unified-visit promise using brand-owned logistics, without warehouse capex. 11 Delivery & Installation Decision Tree

The platform is the single communication layer — not a parallel installation force.

Furniture: own team in the first two launch cities to set the quality bar; certified local partners — trained, platform-branded, never visibly third-party — everywhere else. Appliances: never bypass OEM warranty installation.

Order Confirmed → Installation Routing
flowchart TD A[Order Confirmed] --> B{Product Category} B -->|Furniture| C{Which City?} C -->|Launch Metro| D[Own Installer Team] C -->|Other City| E[Certified Local Partner] B -->|Appliance| F[OEM Installation Triggered] F --> G["Platform Coordinates &
Tracks Against Own SLA"] G --> H["Customer Never Contacts
Brand Directly"] D --> I[Customer Sign-Off] E --> I H --> I
The platform becomes the single communication layer — triggering the request, tracking it against its own SLA, absorbing all customer communication.
Assumption worth challenging: furniture and OEM appliance installation don't need to converge into one visit every time. Forcing an OEM technician onto a furniture installer's schedule is the most likely point of operational failure, since OEM dispatch isn't something the platform controls. The honest promise is "your installation is fully coordinated by us" — not "always one visit."

19 Final Recommendation

Unified, multi-brand fulfillment is the defensible asset — not the AI.

The AI is a meaningful discovery improvement, but reliably installing a sofa, TV, and wardrobe from three brands as one coordinated visit with one point of accountability is operationally hard to replicate — which is exactly why it should be the company's sharpest capability, not an afterthought.

Build First
The four load-bearing pieces
Unified cart and checkout, AI intake-to-recommendation with 3–4 real alternatives, single order/installation tracking, and self-service post-purchase actions. These four make "one customer, one order, one experience" structurally true, not just visible.
Why These Calls
Capital discipline, not feature lists
Every V0 inclusion is load-bearing for unification. Model C over Model A applies the same capital discipline — prove the promise before paying for the warehouse that makes it easier to keep. The OEM-coordination model over forced single-visit installation reflects honest acknowledgment of what the platform actually controls.
What Still Needs Validation
  • Demand for unification — whether customers genuinely value unification enough to choose this over a familiar single-brand purchase.
  • The right amount of choice — whether 3–4 alternatives is the right amount, or customers want more.
  • Logistics coverage in practice — whether Shadowfax's metro coverage and Delhivery's reach combine as cleanly in practice as on paper.
  • Installer quality at scale — whether certified local installers can hold the same bar as the owned team once the model expands beyond the first two cities — the highest-cost assumption to get wrong, since installation quality is the stated differentiator.
